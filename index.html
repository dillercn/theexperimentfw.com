01 — The Concept

A message sent.
A question asked.

In 1977, Carl Sagan's committee faced an impossible task: what should we tell an alien intelligence about humanity? Their answer was the Voyager Golden Record — a 12-inch gold disc carrying music, images, sounds, and greetings in 55 languages, launched into interstellar space aboard Voyager 1 and 2.

Today, Voyager 1 is the most distant human-made object in existence, traveling at 38,000 miles per hour through interstellar space. The record travels with it.

From Earth, With Love asks that same impossible question — to thousands of people, in real time, in a room built specifically to hold it.

Voyager 1 distance from Earth 24,138,540,000 km

"In a sense it's a unifying message. It's a message from Earth. It contains greetings in many languages, music from many cultures, and images that portray our home planet. It's our attempt to say what is Earth — and it's a record of who we think we are."

— Ed Stone, Voyager Project Scientist, JPL

The Institution

From Earth, With Love is the first installation of The Experiment — a new kind of cultural institution that takes a single significant human object and builds an immersive, participatory experience around it. Each installation is a living laboratory. EX.001 is the beginning.

02 — The Experience

Six rooms.
One hour.
An impossible question.

Entry
Threshold

The question is posed. You receive your badge. You are no longer a visitor — you are a participant.

Orientation
Room 01
The Dark Room

Near-complete darkness. Blind Willie Johnson plays. A ceiling projection opens above you. You are invited to lie on the floor.

Contemplation
Room 02
The Choice Room

An illuminated table. Forty etched acrylic symbols. You must choose what represents humanity — and leave things out. Your choices are recorded.

Research / Data
Room 03
The Heartbeat Room

Listen to your own heartbeat. Hold a pulse sensor. Watch the stars on every wall pulse with your rhythm. Leave with a printout of your heartbeat — dated, yours.

Centerpiece
Room 04
Archive & Transmission

What Sagan's committee sent in 1977. Then — what everyone who walked through before you chose. A living visualization, updated continuously.

Collective Data
Room 05
Epilogue

Quiet. Warm. One question on the wall. You write something on a card and add it to thousands of others. You return your badge. You leave.

Integration

From Earth, with love.

Etched into the exit

03 — The Research

A living laboratory.
Real questions.

From Earth, With Love is not only an experience. It is a behavioral research platform. RFID badges, overhead cameras, and interactive data capture generate a continuous dataset across the installation's entire run.

In partnership with academic research collaborators, we study how humans make meaning when asked to represent all of humanity — and what that reveals about who we are right now.

The data grows across the full run, and eventually across cities, demographics, and time. The long-term goal is to travel the installation — and watch how the answers change.

"Do people include symbols of conflict and suffering, or do they default to optimism?"

Behavioral / Values

"Does the answer change after a major world event?"

Longitudinal

"How does making choices on behalf of all humanity change how you see yourself?"

Existential / Psychology

"What does Fort Worth say? What would Berlin say? What would Lagos say?"

Geographic / Cultural

04 — About

Built by someone who has spent twenty years inside the institutions this is trying to reimagine.

The Experiment was created by Nikki Diller, a museum executive with nearly two decades of experience at Texas cultural institutions. The project grows out of a simple conviction: that people deserve experiences that take them seriously, ask them real questions, and actually want to know the answers.

Traditional museums struggle to engage modern audiences in the way they deserve. Immersive entertainment offers spectacle without substance. The Experiment is an attempt to build what should exist between those two things.
